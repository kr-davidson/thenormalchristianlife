Two Goats,
One Atonement

✦

The Sacrifice of Christ, the Cry of Dereliction, and the Double Reconciliation of Leviticus 16

About This Paper

This paper was developed through an extended theological exploration using artificial intelligence as a research, dialogue, and writing partner. The underlying questions, themes, theological concerns, and editorial direction originated through engagement with Scripture, Watchman Nee's The Normal Christian Life, Leviticus 16, and broader questions surrounding atonement, propitiation, expiation, reconciliation, and union with Christ. The paper was generated through iterative dialogue, refinement, critique, and editorial oversight.

It should therefore be understood as an AI-assisted theological reflection rather than a traditional academic publication.

Preface

Preface

This paper grows out of a question that began simply enough: what does the scapegoat of Leviticus 16 have to do with the cross of Jesus Christ?

The question, it turns out, is not simple at all. It reaches into the deepest mechanics of how atonement works, why Paul separates the language of blood from the language of the cross, what Watchman Nee understood when he structured The Normal Christian Life the way he did, and why the cry of dereliction from Calvary is not merely an expression of suffering but a theological event of the first order.

This paper is written for the serious reader who does not need to be protected from difficult ideas but who also does not want to wade through academic apparatus to find them. The argument is advanced. The language, I hope, is clear. The goal throughout is not novelty but coherence: to show that what looks like a tension in the biblical text is in fact a precision, and that when Leviticus 16 is read with the care it deserves, the atonement becomes not simpler but larger.

Section I

The Problem We Begin With

A Mechanism Mismatch

Anyone reading the New Testament carefully will notice that its writers use two distinct and seemingly different categories to describe what happened at the cross when dealing with sin.

The first category is death. Romans 6 is its clearest expression. Sin is dealt with because the person who was enslaved to it died. Not that sin was removed to a safe distance, not that it was forgiven and left in place, but that the old self was crucified, buried, and a new self raised in its place. The mechanism here is execution and resurrection. Sin loses its dominion not because it has been relocated but because its host has died.

The second category is removal. The great Old Testament image here is Psalm 103:12: as far as the east is from the west, so far has He removed our transgressions from us. The direction of movement is outward, away, gone. Sin is not executed; it is expelled.

These look like different models. And in a sense they are. But the question is whether they are competing models or complementary ones. Leviticus 16, read with precision, gives us the answer.

The Two Animals That Are One Offering

The Day of Atonement described in Leviticus 16 is one of the most carefully choreographed rituals in all of Scripture. At its centre are two goats, designated together as a single sin offering (Leviticus 16:5), yet treated in radically different ways.

The first goat is slaughtered. Its blood is collected and carried by the High Priest alone into the Holy of Holies, the innermost chamber of the Tabernacle, where it is sprinkled on the kapporeth, the mercy seat, the covering of the Ark of the Covenant. This act takes place entirely out of sight of the congregation. Israel waits outside. They cannot see what is happening. The entire transaction is between the High Priest and God.

The second goat is not killed. The High Priest emerges from the sanctuary, lays both hands on the head of this living goat, and confesses over it all the iniquities of the people of Israel. The goat is then led away into the wilderness by a designated man and released. It carries the sins of the nation into a desolate place, to Azazel, and does not return.

What we have, then, is not two separate atonements but one atonement with two irreducible movements. The first is Godward and hidden. The second is humanward and visible. Together they constitute the full picture of what it means for sin to be dealt with.

The blood answers what God requires. The scapegoat announces what has consequently become true for the people. Neither is complete without the other. And the order is not interchangeable: blood must be shed before sin can be sent away.

Section II

What the Blood Does and Does Not Do

Watchman Nee's Three Directions

Watchman Nee, in the opening chapter of The Normal Christian Life, establishes what is perhaps the most precise popular treatment of the blood of Christ available in evangelical literature. He does not organise it in formal academic categories, but the logic is unmistakable: the blood operates in three directions, and the order in which it operates those directions is not arbitrary.

The first direction is Godward. The blood of Christ answers the righteousness of God. Before anything else is possible, before any human experience of forgiveness or peace, before any claim can be made on the accuser's silence, God must be satisfied. The blood of Christ does this. It is the propitiation, the hilasterion of Romans 3:25, the mercy seat on which divine wrath and divine mercy meet. Nee's insistence, drawing on Hebrews, is that this transaction does not depend on whether you feel forgiven. It depends on whether God has accepted the blood. And He has.

The second direction is manward. Once the Godward foundation is established, the blood turns toward the conscience of the believer. Hebrews 9:14 is the key text: the blood of Christ, through the eternal Spirit, cleanses the conscience from dead works to serve the living God. This is not therapy. It is not primarily about emotional relief, though relief may follow. It is about the restoration of priestly access.

The third direction is Satanward. Revelation 12:10–11 describes the accuser of the brethren being overcome by the blood of the Lamb. The accusations the enemy levels are not groundless fabrications; they are legal claims based on genuine guilt. What silences them is not denial but payment. The blood removes the legal ground on which accusation stands.

Mapping the Blood onto Leviticus 16

What is striking, once Nee's three directions are clear, is that they map almost entirely onto the first goat. Every movement of the blood operates through the mechanism of death and sacrifice.

The Godward direction corresponds to the blood sprinkled on the mercy seat: a hidden transaction between the High Priest and God, conducted inside the Holy of Holies, independent of any congregational awareness or participation.

The manward direction corresponds to what Israel received when the High Priest emerged: not a sight of blood, but an assurance, grounded in the priestly act completed on their behalf, that the barrier between them and God had been removed.

The Satanward direction has no direct counterpart in the Levitical liturgy, but its logic is embedded in the whole: when the ground of guilt is removed, accusation loses its authority.

All three directions of the blood, as Nee presents them, operate through the mechanism of the sin offering goat: death, blood, satisfaction of holiness, and the consequent silencing of every claim against the worshipper. The scapegoat is absent from this analysis. That is not an oversight. It is a precision. The scapegoat is doing something else entirely.

Section III

What the Scapegoat Does

A Different Question

The sin offering goat answers the question: what does God require before sin can be addressed? The answer is death. Blood must be shed. Holiness must be satisfied.

The scapegoat answers a different question entirely: where has the sin gone, and how do we know it will not return?

These are not the same question, and they do not have the same answer. The blood establishes the ground. The scapegoat declares the consequence. Both are necessary for the full reality of atonement to be communicated, and the sequence between them is theologically mandatory: the scapegoat cannot precede the blood. Sin cannot be sent away before the ground on which it is being addressed has been established.

The Ritual of Transfer

When the High Priest emerges from the Holy of Holies, the sacrificial work is complete. Now he does something that involves, symbolically, the whole nation. He lays both hands on the head of the living goat. In Hebrew ritual, the laying on of hands signifies transfer and identification. Over the goat, the High Priest then confesses all the iniquities of the children of Israel, and all their transgressions in all their sins, putting them on the head of the goat (Leviticus 16:21). The Hebrew verb nasa carries the sense of lifting, bearing, carrying away. The sins do not merely touch the goat; they are placed on it, loaded onto it, and it departs bearing them.

Confessional, Not Constitutive

At this point a distinction must be drawn carefully, because a great deal depends on getting it right. The laying on of hands and the confession of sins do not produce the atonement. They do not cause the transfer to happen in some ontological sense. What the confessional act does is enact, visibly and corporately, the recognition of what is true.

The confession is not what makes the sin go. It is what makes the going of the sin real to the one who has been forgiven. The difference between these two things is the difference between works and faith.

This distinction maps directly onto the New Testament. John 1:12 is the hinge: to all who received Him, who believed in His name, He gave the right to become children of God. The word translated received is elabon, the Greek verb for taking or receiving. It is not a creative act. It does not produce what it receives. It lays hold of what already exists and makes it one's own. This is faith. And this is the scapegoat's posture, translated into the New Testament register.

Section IV

Christ and the Scapegoat: The Active Taking

He Chose to Bear

One of the most important theological affirmations about the cross is that it was not imposed on Christ from outside but embraced by Him from within. John 10:18 is unambiguous: no one takes my life from me, but I lay it down of my own accord. The incarnation was voluntary. The submission to the Father's will in Gethsemane was voluntary. And the taking on of sin was voluntary.

Isaiah 53:6 says that the Lord laid on Him the iniquity of us all. But the same chapter says He bore the sin of many and made intercession for the transgressors (Isaiah 53:12). The passive and active dimensions are both present: it was laid on Him, and He bore it. He was not merely a passive vessel into whom sin was poured. He was the willing bearer who took what was placed and carried it to the place it needed to go.

The Transfer and Its Cost

Second Corinthians 5:21 is one of the densest theological statements in the Pauline corpus: God made Him who knew no sin to be sin for us, that we might become the righteousness of God in Him. The Greek here is stark. Christ was made sin. Not a sinner. Not sinful. Sin itself, in some sense that the text refuses to domesticate. What did Christ's bearing of sin mean for Christ? It meant removal from the presence of the Father.

The Cry of Dereliction as Theological Event

Matthew 27:46 records the moment: about the ninth hour Jesus cried out with a loud voice, Eli, Eli, lema sabachthani? which means, My God, My God, why have you forsaken me? The position that does most justice to both the text and the theology is this: the cry is real, it describes a genuine rupture in the experienced relationship between Father and Son, and it is the direct consequence of what Christ was bearing.

When sin came upon Christ, the One who is of purer eyes than to behold evil (Habakkuk 1:13) turned away. Not because the Father stopped loving the Son, but because the sin the Son was bearing required the same consequence it had always required: separation from the holy God.

Christ did not merely die. He was forsaken. And the forsakenness was the scapegoat's wilderness entered, not from the outside in, but from the inside of the Godhead out.

Section V

Two Reconciliations, Not One

The Problem Was Never Only One Thing

When Paul describes the human condition apart from Christ, he uses two distinct categories that correspond precisely to the two problems the Day of Atonement was designed to address. The first is guilt. The second is separation. Ephesians 2:12 describes the condition as separated from Christ, alienated from the commonwealth of Israel, strangers to the covenants of promise, having no hope and without God in the world. These two problems are related, because it is sin that produces both the guilt and the separation. But they are not identical, and their resolution is correspondingly double.

Reconciled from Judgment

The first reconciliation addresses guilt. Christ as High Priest takes His own blood into the presence of the Father and presents it as the satisfaction of divine justice. Romans 3:25–26 states this with legal precision: God put forward Christ as a propitiation to demonstrate His righteousness, so that He would be just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus. The blood speaks. It speaks to God's righteousness on our behalf. It silences accusation because it answers the charge rather than dismissing it.

Reconciled to Presence

The second reconciliation addresses the separation. If sin created exile from the presence of God, then the removal of sin restores access to that presence. Colossians 1:21–22 captures this precisely: you who once were alienated and hostile in mind, doing evil deeds, He has now reconciled in His body of flesh by His death, in order to present you holy and blameless and above reproach before Him. The movement is from alienation to presentation. From outside to inside. From the wilderness to the presence.

He went into the wilderness of Godforsakenness bearing our sin, and He came back. The resurrection is not an afterthought; it is the return of the sin-bearer from the place of exile, and His return without the sin He bore is the announcement that the exile is over for those who are in Him.

Section VI

What Paul Adds That Leviticus Cannot Reach

The Dominion Problem

The Day of Atonement addresses two things: the guilt of sin and the separation caused by sin. What it does not address, and was never designed to address, is the power of sin as a reigning principle within the person. This is the problem Romans 6 is written to solve. And it is striking that when Paul takes up this problem, he does not reach for any sacrificial image at all. He speaks of death and burial and resurrection. The old man, he says, was crucified with Christ (Romans 6:6). We were buried therefore with Him by baptism into death (Romans 6:4).

Why the Shift in Mechanism Matters

This is not a contradiction between Paul and the Levitical system. It is an extension. The Day of Atonement dealt with sins — the discrete acts of transgression that accumulated as a corporate burden on the nation. Romans 6 deals with Sin in the singular, the governing power that produces the acts of transgression and that holds the person in bondage regardless of how many individual sins are forgiven.

You can forgive every debt a slave has incurred without setting the slave free. The debt is cleared, but the condition of bondage remains. What is needed is not just forgiveness of debt but liberation from the condition that produces debt. Nee understood this precisely, which is why The Normal Christian Life moves in the sequence it does.

The blood answers the question: what has been done about what I have done? The cross answers the question: what has been done about what I am?

Section VII

The Irreversibility of Both Acts

Two Kinds of Permanence

One of the deepest pastoral fruits of understanding Leviticus 16 correctly is the recognition that the atonement it enacts has an irreversible directionality on both sides.

The blood of Christ, Hebrews tells us, continues to speak. Unlike the blood of Abel, which cried out for vengeance (Hebrews 12:24), the blood of Christ speaks better things. It speaks permanently, interceding before the Father in the ongoing High Priesthood of the risen Christ. Christ entered once for all into the holy places, obtaining eternal redemption (Hebrews 9:12).

The scapegoat, by contrast, departed. It did not circle back. It went into the wilderness of Azazel and did not return. Psalm 103:12 maps this spatially: as far as the east is from the west, so far has He removed our transgressions from us. East and west, on a sphere, never converge.

Why the Accuser Has No Ground

This double permanence is precisely what dismantles the logic of accusation. Revelation 12:10–11 describes the accuser as one who accuses the brethren before God day and night, and who is overcome by the blood of the Lamb. The accuser's argument is always the same: these people are guilty, and guilt has consequences. What the blood answers is not the facts but the legal resolution of the facts. The charge has been met in full. There is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus (Romans 8:1). And the accuser cannot retrieve sins that have been carried to the place of no return.

Section VIII

Faith as Reception

The Human Posture

Throughout this paper, the atonement has been described primarily from the side of what God has done. This is intentional, and it reflects the priority of the biblical text. The initiative is entirely divine. But there is a human posture that corresponds to this divine act, and its character is significant.

The congregation of Israel did not passively ignore the Day of Atonement. They gathered. They afflicted their souls (Leviticus 16:29), an expression of humility and acknowledgment. And they watched the scapegoat depart. The watching was not incidental. It was the designed mechanism by which the reality of the removal became real to them.

The New Testament equivalent is faith. And John 1:12 names it with precision: to all who received Him, who believed in His name, He gave the right to become children of God. The word received, elabon, is the key. Receiving is not creating. It is not earning. It is the act of taking hold of what is being given, of allowing what has already been done to become yours.

The Scapegoat and Salvation by Faith

The scapegoat ritual has, embedded within it, a picture of exactly this posture. The community did not earn the removal of sins by their quality of confession. What the ritual required of Israel was recognition and reception: the acknowledgment that these are our sins, and the willingness to see them depart on the one who bears them.

Christ chose to bear our sins. We did not commission Him to do so. We were not even aware He was doing it. But in the mystery of grace, we are invited, after the fact, to place our hands on what He has done, to confess it as ours, and to watch it depart with Him into the wilderness of Godforsakenness, never to be charged to us again.

Conclusion

Three Problems, Three Solutions, One Salvation

We began with what looked like a mechanism mismatch. The blood deals with guilt by death. The scapegoat deals with sin by removal. Romans 6 deals with the old man by execution and new creation. These appear to be three different answers to three different problems.

They are. And that is precisely the point. The gospel is not a single transaction but a comprehensive salvation. The atonement accomplished by Christ addresses every dimension of the human problem, and Leviticus 16, read with care, already contains the structure of what the New Testament will fully articulate.

The Problem The Solution The Mechanism The Biblical Locus
Guilt before God Propitiation Blood of the sin offering goat; Christ's death as sacrifice Romans 3:25; Hebrews 9; Rev 12:11
Separation from God's presence Expiation and reconciliation The scapegoat bearing sin away; Christ's Godforsakenness and resurrection return 2 Cor 5:21; Matt 27:46; Col 1:21–22
Dominion of sin within Liberation through death and new creation Co-crucifixion; burial; resurrection; union with Christ Romans 6; Galatians 2:20

The blood satisfies God and cleanses the conscience, establishing the objective ground on which every claim of guilt is answered. The scapegoat declares the irreversible removal of what caused separation, and the believer receives this through the confessional act of faith. The cross, understood as the death of the old man in union with Christ, addresses the power of sin at its root, not by relocating it but by killing the one it ruled.

Three solutions. But one Saviour who accomplished them all: the High Priest who carried His own blood into the presence of God, the willing sin-bearer who entered the wilderness of dereliction bearing what we laid on Him, and the crucified and risen Lord in whom the old man died and the new creation lives.

The atonement is not a single note but a chord. And Leviticus 16 gives us the score.

Soli Deo Gloria
