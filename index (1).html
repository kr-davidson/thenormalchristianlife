Leviticus 16, the Two Goats, Watchman Nee, and the Atonement

About This Document

This document records the theological exploration and dialogue that ultimately contributed to the development of the reflection paper Two Goats, One Atonement. It is presented for readers who wish to understand the development of the argument and the questions that shaped the final paper.

The dialogue is presented as it occurred, between the site author (K.R.) and an AI research and dialogue partner. The questions, challenges, and editorial direction originated with K.R. The AI responses provided theological research, synthesis, and structured analysis in response to those questions.

✦   ✦   ✦

Opening: The Mechanism Mismatch

K.R.

I'm looking at Leviticus 16, and I'm looking at the scapegoat. And I'm trying to see if there is a correlation in either John Stott's theology — specifically, I'm looking to speak to chapter 2 on Watchman Nee's The Normal Christian Life — and the cross. And I'm happy to take a look at how Luther dealt with this, but I'm trying to take a look at the scapegoat and the removal of sin because under Romans 6, I only see the removal coming by way of death, not by way of the removal from the presence of God. Although I often think that the removal from the presence of God is death. Please challenge some of my thinking to bring it into coherence theologically.

Research Response

The Core Tension

You're noticing what looks like a mechanism mismatch: Romans 6 — sin's removal comes through death (co-crucifixion with Christ, burial, resurrection to new life). Leviticus 16 scapegoat — sin's removal comes through banishment (carried away into the wilderness, away from the camp, not killed). And your instinct is sharp: these look like two different models. Death vs. distance.

What Leviticus 16 Actually Gives Us — Two Animals, Not One

This is the key that often gets missed. The Day of Atonement uses two goats functioning as a single offering (16:5 — "one sin offering"): the first goat is slaughtered — its blood brought into the Holy of Holies (propitiatory movement: satisfying the holiness of God). The scapegoat has the sins confessed over it and is sent away — never killed (expiation/removal movement: the visible, enacted declaration that sin has been carried away). So Leviticus 16 itself already contains both a death and a removal. They are two aspects of one atonement, not two competing models.

Where Stott and Luther Help

Stott insists that propitiation and expiation must be held together. The cross is simultaneously God's wrath satisfied (propitiation — the blood on the mercy seat) and sin removed from the record and the presence (expiation — the scapegoat's departure). Luther approached the scapegoat through his theology of substitution — Christ becomes the "accused one," bearing the totality of sin's weight and removing it by bearing it away from us. For Luther the wilderness is exile, and Christ goes into the exile that belonged to us — the godforsaken place. His cry of dereliction is precisely this: the experience of being expelled from the presence while bearing our sin.

Your Sharpest Instinct — "Removal from God's Presence IS Death"

You are right that in biblical theology, separation from God is the essence of death. Genesis 3 — the expulsion from the Garden is the outworking of "you shall surely die." Death in Scripture is not primarily biological cessation; it is relational rupture with the source of life. So exile from the presence = death. Your instinct is deeply covenantal and correct.

But here's the complication worth sitting with: Romans 6 doesn't just say sin is removed or forgiven — it says the self that was defined by sin died. The old man is not merely sent away; it is executed. This is categorically stronger than the scapegoat. The scapegoat carries sin into the wilderness — the goat itself survives. But Paul's logic in Romans 6 is that you died.

Leviticus 16 Theological Function Pauline Correspondence
First goat — slaughtered, blood on mercy seat Propitiation; satisfies divine holiness Romans 3:25; Romans 6 (death with Christ)
Scapegoat — sins confessed, sent to wilderness Expiation; removal of sin from the community Romans 8:1 (no condemnation); Psalm 103:12

Nee's Chapter One: Mapping the Two Goats

K.R.

Looking at Watchman Nee's chapter one on the blood of Christ, how would you map the sin offering goat vs. the scapegoat?

Research Response

What Nee Does in Chapter One

Nee's central distinction in chapter one is between forgiveness and cleansing — but more precisely, he is establishing that the blood of Christ deals with the problem of sin before God, not sin as experienced by the conscience primarily, but sin as it stands as an objective problem before the holiness of God. His key move is this: the blood is not primarily about your feeling of forgiveness. It is about what God sees.

Mapping the Two Goats onto Nee's Chapter One Framework

The sin offering goat maps directly and precisely onto Nee's chapter one. The slaughtered goat's blood is brought by the High Priest into the Holy of Holies — the audience for this act is God alone. No Israelite sees it happen. It is entirely transacted in the divine presence. This is exactly Nee's point — the blood speaks to God, not to us.

The scapegoat, by contrast, does not map cleanly onto chapter one. The scapegoat's movement is toward the people, not toward God. If chapter one is Nee's Godward foundation, the scapegoat belongs to a different moment — closer to what Nee will develop later around the believer's assurance and reckoning.

Element Leviticus 16 Nee Chapter One
Sin offering goat Slaughtered; blood to mercy seat; Godward; unseen by people Blood of Christ speaking to God; objective; basis of God's satisfaction
Scapegoat Sins confessed over it; sent to wilderness; visible; humanward Belongs to later chapters on reckoning and assurance
Order Blood first, then removal Foundation first, then experience
Audience God (first goat); Israel (scapegoat) God's side (ch.1); believer's side (ch.2 onward)

Nee's Two Movements in Chapter One

K.R.

But doesn't Watchman Nee in chapter one also deal with the blood such that the first part is manward, addressing the guilty conscience? Is that not synonymous with the scapegoat and the communal appreciation of atonement?

Research Response

You Are Correct — Nee's Chapter One Has Two Movements

Nee does not begin with the Godward side. He actually begins with the guilty conscience. So the structure of chapter one is: Problem stated — manward (the troubled conscience, the lack of peace); Diagnosis: the believer has been looking at their own feelings rather than at what God sees; Solution — Godward: the blood speaks to God; God's side is the foundation; Return — manward: because God is satisfied, the conscience can be settled.

Does That Make the Opening Move Synonymous with the Scapegoat?

The scapegoat addresses the community's visible, public, enacted assurance that sin has been removed. Israel watches the goat disappear into the wilderness. Nee's opening manward movement is addressing the individual conscience — specifically the believer who is trapped in repetitive confession, unable to rest. The question is personal assurance, not communal declaration.

Scapegoat Nee's Opening Manward Move
Audience Corporate — all Israel sees Individual — the troubled believer
Mode Visible, enacted, dramatic sign Internal, experiential, conscience-level
Direction Sin carried away from the camp Peace sought for the guilty conscience
Resolution Public declaration accomplished Redirecting conscience to God's objective satisfaction

The Three Directions of the Blood

K.R.

Nee's chapter one has the blood dealing with three aspects in this order: Godward, Manward, and Satanward. The blood first satisfies God, then secures the believer's conscience, and thereby silences every remaining accusation. This seems to separate the two goats and focus just on the sin offering. It leads me to believe that the scapegoat, while still being one act of atonement, is tied more to the Cross and the taking on of sin — which removes Christ from the presence of God — and that the death required from sinning.

Research Response

Affirming What You Have Established

You are right that Nee's three-part structure — Godward, Manward, Satanward — maps almost entirely onto the sin offering goat, not the scapegoat. All three movements are about what the blood accomplishes: before God (propitiation), before the conscience (cleansing), before the accuser (silencing). None of these require the scapegoat's mechanism of removal by distance.

The Scapegoat as Christ's Godforsakenness

The scapegoat in Leviticus 16 bears the sins (nasa in Hebrew — to carry, to lift and take away), goes into the wilderness (desolate, uninhabited, outside the covenant camp), and goes to Azazel (the place of the utterly outside, the realm of desolation). Now consider what happens to Christ at the moment of dereliction: He bears sin (Isaiah 53:6; 2 Corinthians 5:21), He is cast into desolation relationally and experientially, and He cries Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani. The structural parallel is exact.

Sin Offering Goat Scapegoat
What it depicts Christ's death satisfying divine holiness Christ's bearing of sin into Godforsakenness
Nee's application Blood — Godward, Manward, Satanward Not in chapter one — belongs to the cost Christ bore
Pauline text Romans 3:25; Hebrews 9 2 Corinthians 5:21; Matthew 27:46
Direction Toward God — propitiation Away from God — expulsion, desolation
What it answers How is God satisfied? What did bearing sin cost Christ relationally?

The Scapegoat and the Laying On of Sins

K.R.

I still think the scapegoat is separate from the blood. In Revelation 12:11 the Satanward is dealt with by the blood of the Lamb. I think the scapegoat is the laying on of our sins on Christ on the cross, and when we lay our sins on Him, it is this that removes Him from the presence, and makes atonement for us. Where we were separated because of our sins, we are reconciled in two ways: from God's judgment where death was our penalty, and restored to His presence from the removal of sin. The first He presents to the Lord as our High Priest, the mediator before God. The second He takes on for the body and this is our part of the process where we confess what has occurred. The scapegoat seems to be our recognition that we have sinned and that we have put these on Him and that is what caused Him being removed from the presence of God.

Research Response

Stating Your Position Precisely

You are proposing that the two goats represent two distinct but inseparable acts within the one atonement: the sin offering goat — Christ as High Priest presents His own blood before the Father (entirely His act, Godward, mediatorial); the scapegoat — a participatory act involving the congregation. The laying on of hands, the confession over the goat, the transfer of sins. And it is this — the bearing of our sin — that caused His separation from the Father.

What Is Exegetically Strong Here

Your reading of Leviticus 16 is liturgically careful. The two rituals genuinely do have different human postures. The sin offering — the High Priest acts alone. He goes in. No one else participates. The scapegoat — the High Priest comes out and the community is actively involved through the representative laying on of hands and confession.

A Genuine Challenge

Your proposal places significant theological weight on the human act of confession and laying on of hands as constitutive of what causes Christ's separation from the Father. This raises a question: Is the sin transfer the cause of the Godforsakenness, or is the Godforsakenness the condition Christ entered in order to accomplish the sin transfer? These are not the same thing, and the direction matters enormously.

The Liturgical Order as Evidence

Leviticus 16 may actually help resolve this. The laying on of hands does not happen until after the sin offering goat has already been slaughtered and the blood presented. This suggests the order is: (1) Death and blood — the objective ground is laid; (2) Then and only then — the sins are laid on the scapegoat and carried away. Which would mean the Godforsakenness is consequent on the completed propitiatory act, not produced by the confession itself.

Two Forms of Reconciliation

Your point about two forms of reconciliation is theologically precise: reconciled from judgment — restored from the penalty, through the blood, the High Priest's work Godward; reconciled to presence — restored from the separation, through the removal of sin that caused the rupture. These are genuinely distinct and both are necessary for full salvation.

Confessional, Not Constitutive: John 1:12

K.R.

I concur that our position is not constitutive but confessional. Christ chose and took on our sins. We were not even aware that He was doing this. There is, however, a confessional recognition of this. I am thinking of John 1:12 — that we need to recognize Him and what He did. We must receive this. That this is salvation by faith.

Research Response

What You Have Now Established

The two goats are not two mechanisms competing for explanatory priority. They are one atonement seen from two angles, and the human posture toward each is fundamentally different: the sin offering goat — Christ acting as High Priest, presenting His blood before the Father. Entirely His initiative, entirely Godward, entirely prior to any human awareness or response. The scapegoat — Christ bearing our sins and carrying them into Godforsakenness. Again, entirely His initiative. But this act has a confessional structure that the sin offering does not — not because our confession produces it, but because this is the dimension of atonement that requires a response of recognition and reception to be personally appropriated.

John 1:12 as the New Testament Scapegoat Moment

John 1:12 is exactly the right text to anchor this. The word received — elabon — is the key. It is not productive. It is receptive. But it is not passive either. There is a genuine act of receiving, of recognition, of faith that lays hold of what has already been accomplished.

Leviticus 16 Scapegoat John 1:12
Sins laid on the goat by the High Priest Christ took our sins — His initiative entirely
Laying on of hands — confessional recognition We receive Him — faith, recognition, reception
The goat departs bearing sin Sin carried away, separation healed
Israel watches and knows — it is gone The believer knows — I have received Him, sin is gone
Restoration to the camp, to community, to presence Right to become children of God — restored to presence

Paul's Separation of Blood and Cross

K.R.

I concur that Paul separates the blood from the cross and this speaks specifically to the necessity of the death of the old man and not just sacrifice. It appears what might be additive in Nee's Chapter 1 is the expiation which follows the propitiation. It appears that Leviticus adds this confessional dimension that is associated with the removal of sins into the wilderness. It is important, though, to recognize that the blood addresses the cleansing of the conscience, and the blood addresses our ability to come before God. The casting of our iniquities onto Him is a confessional act, not a constitutive act.

Research Response

The Clean Theological Structure You Have Reached

Propitiation — the blood — sin offering goat: This is the foundation and it does two things that are distinct but inseparable. Godward — satisfies divine holiness, removes the ground of judgment, justification. Manward — cleanses the conscience, restores access, enables approach to God without condemnation. Hebrews 9:14 holds both together precisely — the blood cleanses the conscience so that we may serve the living God. This is entirely Christ's act, entirely prior, entirely Godward in its foundation even when applied manward in its effect.

Expiation — the scapegoat — the confessional dimension: This is genuinely additive to propitiation, not redundant with it. It answers a different question: not how is God satisfied but where has the sin gone and how do I know it is gone. And critically — as you have precisely identified — the human posture here is confessional, not constitutive.

What Leviticus Adds That Is Otherwise Easy to Miss

The two goats show that atonement has an irreversible directionality: the blood remains before God — speaking, interceding, satisfying (Hebrews 12:24); the sin departs from us — removed, carried away, irreversible. Both permanences are necessary for full assurance.

Chapter Problem Addressed Mechanism Leviticus Analog
One — blood Guilt, conscience, accusation Propitiation and expiation Both goats
Two onward — cross Dominion of sin, the old man Death and resurrection, union with Christ Neither goat — new creation

The Clean Synthesis

Research Response — Final Synthesis

One Sentence That Holds the Whole

The blood satisfies God and cleanses the conscience so we can come before Him. The scapegoat declares the irreversible removal of what separated us, received through confession and faith. The cross kills the one who would sin again. Three distinct problems. Three distinct solutions. One coherent salvation.

The Structure of the Full Argument

This synthesis, developed through the dialogue above, became the structural backbone of the paper Two Goats, One Atonement. The paper developed each of these three movements in full, adding the analysis of the cry of dereliction, the double reconciliation, and the relationship between Paul's treatment of the old man and the Levitical system.
