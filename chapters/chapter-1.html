The Blood
of Christ

✦

This chapter addresses the most foundational question a person can face: How can a sinful human being stand accepted before a holy God? Watchman Nee begins not with spiritual growth or Christian character, but with guilt and forgiveness. His answer is clear and uncompromising. The basis of our standing before God is not our feelings, our efforts, or our progress. It is the blood of Jesus Christ.

The Central Truth of This Chapter

My forgiveness before God rests entirely on the blood of Christ, not on my feelings or my performance.

Many believers carry a burden that quietly undermines their entire Christian life. It is not simply the weight of their sins, though that is real enough. It is a deeper, more persistent problem: they are uncertain whether God truly receives them. They feel the accusation of their conscience. They look at their inconsistent behaviour and wonder whether their standing with God is truly secure.

Watchman Nee understood this struggle. He knew that a Christian cannot grow, cannot experience freedom, and cannot walk with confidence if the foundational question of acceptance before God remains unresolved. This is why he begins here, at the very beginning, with the blood of Christ.

The chapter does not offer comfort by minimising sin. It does not offer reassurance by pointing to a believer's progress or sincerity. It offers something far more substantial: an objective, unchanging ground of acceptance that rests entirely outside of us, in what Christ has done.

The Starting Point

Christianity does not begin with self-improvement. It begins with reconciliation. Before a person can grow in Christ, they must be at peace with God. And that peace rests not on their own efforts, but on what Christ accomplished at the cross.

✦   ✦   ✦

Before the Christian life can be lived at all, a problem must be addressed. It is the problem of guilt. Every human being, regardless of how decent or well-intentioned, has committed specific acts of wrongdoing. These acts accumulate as a record before a God who is entirely holy. This is not a secondary concern to be managed alongside spiritual growth. It is the first and most urgent problem.

Nee does not allow the reader to begin with something more comfortable, such as living victoriously or becoming a mature Christian. He insists that the first question is this: Have your sins been dealt with before God? Unless they have, nothing else that follows will stand on solid ground.

The Human Problem The Divine Answer
A record of sins before a holy God Forgiveness and cleansing through Christ's blood
Guilt that cannot be undone by effort Justification: declared righteous in Christ
A broken relationship with God Reconciliation: the barrier removed
An accusing conscience A cleansed conscience through Christ's work
The Root Issue

The problem is not primarily that we need to become better people. The problem is that we stand guilty before a holy God. Christianity addresses this root issue first, and the blood of Christ is God's answer.

✦   ✦   ✦

Nee makes a distinction that runs throughout the entire book. It is simple, but it is important to hold onto from the beginning. The words sins and sin are not used interchangeably in the New Testament, and understanding the difference helps a great deal.

Sins (plural) Sin (singular)
Specific acts of wrongdoing we commit The deeper inward condition of our nature
What we do that is wrong What we are apart from God's grace
These produce guilt before God This produces bondage and spiritual defeat
Chapter 1 focuses here: the blood of Christ answers this problem Later chapters: the cross of Christ deals with this deeper question

This chapter is primarily concerned with sins: the accumulation of specific wrongs that create a record of guilt before God. The deeper question of sin as a power and condition will be addressed in the chapters that follow.

A Simple Way to Hold This

Sins are the fruit; sin is the root. The blood of Christ deals with the fruit. The cross of Christ, as Nee later explains, deals with the root. Both are necessary for the full Christian life.

✦   ✦   ✦

Nee is careful to show that the blood of Christ is not simply a symbol or a spiritual concept. It is the actual ground upon which God forgives, justifies, and reconciles sinners. The New Testament speaks of the blood of Christ in relation to several distinct but connected realities.

Forgiveness

The blood of Christ is the basis upon which God forgives specific sins. This is not a legal fiction or a divine looking-the-other-way. God's forgiveness is grounded in the fact that Christ bore the penalty for sin in His death.

Ephesians 1:7  •  1 John 1:7

Justification

Justification means that God declares the believer righteous. It is not merely the removal of a guilty verdict. It is the positive declaration that the one who trusts in Christ is accepted and right before God, resting entirely on Christ's merit.

Romans 3:23–25  •  Romans 5:1

Reconciliation

Reconciliation means that the barrier between God and the sinner has been removed. Guilt and estrangement created a wall. Christ's blood removed it. This is objective: it was accomplished at the cross, not produced by our emotional state.

Romans 5:10  •  2 Corinthians 5:19

Cleansing

Beyond the legal dimension, the blood of Christ also addresses the accusing conscience. The writer of Hebrews speaks of how the blood of Christ cleanses the conscience from dead works, enabling the believer to serve the living God.

Hebrews 9:14  •  Hebrews 10:22
On the Word “Objective”

When Christians speak of an “objective” basis for forgiveness, they mean something that exists outside of us and does not depend on our inner state. The blood of Christ was shed in history. It accomplishes what it accomplishes whether we feel it or not. Faith receives this reality; it does not produce it.

✦   ✦   ✦

This is perhaps the most practically important section of the entire chapter. Nee is not dismissing the emotional life of the believer. He is making a more fundamental point.

The problem is this: many Christians unconsciously treat their emotional state as the measure of their standing before God. When they feel close to God, they feel forgiven. When they feel distant or ashamed, they act as though their forgiveness is in question. This makes their assurance entirely dependent on the fluctuations of their inner life.

Key Truth

God's Word is more reliable than my changing feelings. The ground of my acceptance before God does not rise and fall with my emotions.

The conscience in particular can be persistently accusing. A believer may know the gospel intellectually, may have confessed a sin many times, and may still find that the conscience continues to bring charges. The question to ask is not “Do I feel forgiven?” but “Has God said that Christ's blood is sufficient to deal with this sin?”

When I look at myself When I look at Christ
I see inconsistency and failure I see a finished and sufficient work
I feel the accusation of past wrongs I hear God's own word of declaration
I waver according to recent performance His merit and standing never waver
I find reasons for doubt I find grounds for settled confidence
My condition changes from day to day His sacrifice was made once, for all time
A Practical Question

Ask yourself honestly: when you feel close to God, is it because you have been doing well? And when you feel distant, is it because you have been doing poorly? If so, you may be living as though your standing before God is determined by your performance rather than by Christ's.

✦   ✦   ✦

The conscience is a significant subject in this chapter. The writer of Hebrews uses striking language to describe what Christ's blood does. It cleanses the conscience from dead works, enabling the believer to serve the living God Hebrews 9:14. The conscience can genuinely be weighed down by unresolved guilt. And it can genuinely be set free.

But there is an important clarification to make. The conscience is not infallible. A sensitive conscience is a gift, but a well-trained conscience is even more so. The conscience must be educated by Scripture. It must be brought under the truth of what God has declared about the blood of His Son.

This Is Not Indifference to Sin

A cleansed conscience is not a careless one. Freedom from condemnation is not permission to continue in sin. What Nee is describing is the freedom that comes when a person has genuinely confessed before God and rested on Christ's work, and yet the conscience continues to bring accusations. In that moment, the person needs the truth of the gospel more than they need more self-examination.

Hebrews 10:22 speaks of drawing near to God with a true heart in full assurance of faith, with hearts sprinkled clean from an evil conscience. The goal is not a numbed conscience, but a thoroughly cleansed one. And the cleansing comes not from our achieving a certain moral standard, but from the blood of Christ applied in faith.

✦   ✦   ✦

There is a particular kind of spiritual struggle that is not caused by indifference to sin, but by too great a preoccupation with it. The person who spends all of their time examining their own heart, cataloguing their failures, and looking inward for signs of spiritual health can become as spiritually paralysed as the person who never examines themselves at all.

This is the danger of living by introspection. The Christian life is not primarily about achieving a satisfactory inner state. It is about looking to Christ. The believer's gaze must ultimately rest on Christ, not on the self.

Not a Rejection of Repentance

To be clear: Nee is not telling believers to avoid honesty before God. Genuine confession and repentance are necessary and good. The danger he identifies is something different. It is the tendency to make our own inner assessment the final word on our standing before God, rather than allowing God's Word to be that final word.

The Christian life begins by looking away from self to Christ. This is what the Scriptures repeatedly call faith. It is not the intensity of feeling toward Christ. It is the act of trusting what Christ has done, regardless of what our inner state may seem to indicate in a given moment.

✦   ✦   ✦

Guided Reflection

Take time with each question. These are not meant to be answered quickly. Some may require you to sit with them for a while.

Question 1

On what basis, honestly, do I believe that God receives me today? Is it because of how I have been living, or because of what Christ has done?

Question 2

What do I tend to trust most: my feelings about God, my recent performance, or the declared word of God concerning Christ's blood?

Question 3

When I feel accused or ashamed before God, where do I turn first? Do I turn to Christ's finished work, or do I turn inward, looking for evidence that I deserve to be received?

Question 4

Do I believe that the forgiveness Christ offers is complete, or do I secretly feel that it remains partial until I feel genuinely sorry enough, or have improved enough to be truly deserving of it?

Question 5

Is there a particular past sin or pattern of failure that my conscience continues to bring up, even after confession? What would it mean to bring that accusation before the declared sufficiency of Christ's blood?

Question 6

How does my relationship with God change when I have had what I consider a good week spiritually, compared to a week where I have struggled? What does this reveal about my actual ground of assurance?

Question 7

Can I distinguish in my own experience between self-examination that leads me to Christ, and introspection that turns inward and circles without resolution? What does the difference feel like?

Question 8

What would it mean for me to live with a truly settled conscience, one that has been cleansed by Christ's blood and is no longer carrying a burden that He has already borne? What would change in my daily life?

Pause and Pray

Lord Jesus Christ,


I confess that I have too often looked to myself for the assurance that only Your finished work can give. I have trusted my feelings when they were favourable, and doubted Your acceptance when they were not. I have allowed my conscience to carry burdens that You already bore on the cross.


I thank You for Your blood, shed once, sufficient for all my sins. I thank You that my standing before God does not rest on my performance or my feelings, but on what You have accomplished. I receive that now, not because I feel worthy of it, but because You have declared it in Your Word.


Grant me a settled heart. Teach my conscience to rest in the truth of the gospel rather than in the fluctuations of my inner life. Let Your blood be the ground on which I stand before You, now and always.


Amen.

Chapter Summary

The following points gather together the major threads of this chapter.

1
The First Problem Is Guilt. Before the Christian life can be properly lived, the question of guilt before a holy God must be addressed. The Christian life does not start with growth or maturity. It starts with reconciliation.
2
The Blood of Christ Is Sufficient. Christ's blood is the objective ground of forgiveness, justification, reconciliation, and cleansing. These are declared realities, accomplished in history and received by faith.
3
Forgiveness Is Objective. Because the blood of Christ was shed in history, forgiveness is not dependent on our emotional state. It does not fluctuate according to our feelings or our performance.
4
Feelings Are Not the Foundation. It is spiritually dangerous to treat our inner state as the measure of our standing before God. Feelings can be real and meaningful, but they are unreliable guides to the objective truth of our acceptance in Christ.
5
The Conscience Must Be Instructed. The conscience can accuse even when Christ's blood has genuinely dealt with the sin in question. It must be educated by Scripture and brought to rest on Christ's finished work.
6
This Is the Foundation for Everything Else. Without a settled understanding of forgiveness and acceptance through Christ, the rest of the Christian life lacks a stable base.

Looking Ahead

→

Chapter 1 has addressed the problem of sins: specific acts of wrongdoing that produce guilt before God. The blood of Christ is God's answer to this problem.

But there is a deeper question waiting to be addressed. What about the power of sin itself? What about the inward condition that drives the sinful acts in the first place? A person can know their sins are forgiven and yet still feel enslaved to patterns they cannot seem to break.

This is exactly where the next major section of the book goes. Nee will turn from the blood of Christ to the cross of Christ, moving from the question of guilt to the question of power.
